## Fundraising Fundamentals

### The Two Drivers of Intros

**Source:** *The Truth About Fundraising Intros* (Jason Yeh)

Why do some founders get intros and others don't? It comes down to **two things:**

#### 1. Reciprocity
> "Reciprocity is the simplest to understand. People help people who've helped them before."

**Key Insight:**
- The person you sat next to in ECON 101 freshman year now lives in SF, married a VC
- They would gladly intro you if you just asked
- You never thought of them as a valuable node in your fundraise
- **Action:** Do deep network research. LinkedIn data is dirty—take time to mine connections

#### 2. Value
People make intros when:
- It enhances THEIR reputation
- They're confident you'll represent them well
- The founder has built credibility over time

**Critical Quote:**
> "There was a massive gap between hearing that statement ['intros are the lifeblood'] and understanding it enough to drive successful fundraises."

### The Soft Commitment Trap

**Source:** *What Is a Soft Commitment (and Why Founders Shouldn't Trust It)*

- VCs give soft commitments to keep options open
- Soft commits mean NOTHING until term sheet signed
- **Red flag:** "I'm interested" without timeline or specifics
- **Action:** Push for hard commitments or move on

## Angel Army Strategy

### The Core Math

**Source:** *Building an Angel Army* (Jason Yeh)

| Investment | Check Size | Value |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| **Option A:** Dentist | $100k | Cash only |
| **Option B:** Coinbase PM | $1k | Credibility + Network |

**Key Quote:**
> "The added Credibility and Network you derive from your Angel Army is more valuable than the dollars they actually contribute."

### Why Speed Matters

**Traditional approach (10 years):**
- Build relationships organically
- Help people without asking
- Have network ready when it's your turn

**Condensed approach (10 weeks):**
- Allocate capital NOT for operations
- Get the most helpful people on your cap table
- Network compounds: 30 people → 100+ intros → Series A momentum

### Angel Army Tiers

| Tier | Profile | Check Size | Value |
|------|---------|------------|-------|
| **Tier 1: Credibility** | AI influencers, exited founders, ex-FAANG | $5k | Instant legitimacy |
| **Tier 2: Network** | VCs with newsletters, tech journalists | $2.5k | Distribution |
| **Tier 3: Operators** | Engineers at cool companies, PMs, designers | $1k | Talent + advice |

### The "Butter Up" Framework

**Don't say:** "Will you invest in my company?"  
**Say:** "You're exactly the person I want on this team. Your experience at X makes you perfect."

**Pricing Strategy:**
- Target: 2-5% total dilution
- Frame as "joining the team before the mega valuation"
- Create urgency: "This deal won't be available in the full raise"

## Pitch & Deck Mastery

### Template-Free Zone

**Source:** *Fundraising Strategies They Don't Want You To Know (Part 1)*

> "I don't believe in templates. Every story is unique, and your deck should follow the path that best tells your story. Templates can totally kill your creativity and end up making your pitch vanilla and forgettable."

**The Four Themes You MUST Cover:**

1. **The Problem and Opportunity**
   - Make it crystal clear what problem you're solving
   - Why is it deeply painful?

2. **Your Solution**
   - What makes it unique and insightful?
   - What's your unfair advantage?

3. **Potential Impact**
   - VCs look for massive potential
   - Must be FELT within the deck

4. **The Team**
   - Why YOUR team will make it happen?
   - Credibility indicators

### Go Bold or Go Home

**Source:** Real-world example from Jason's client

**The startup:** Porn-related (taboo, risky, unconventional)

**What they did:**
- Did NOT tone it down for squeamish investors
- Those weren't the right investors anyway

**The result:**
- Not everyone loved it
- Those who did were FIRED UP
- Signed checks from aligned investors

**Key Lesson:**
> "When you push hard and go big, you're almost inviting people to disagree with you and not like your idea. But it's way better to be specific and bold rather than watering down your message."

## Investor Psychology

### Understanding VC Signals (Part 1 & 2)

**Source:** *Understanding VC Signals*

VCs communicate through:
- Response time (fast = interested)
- Meeting frequency (multiple meetings = good)
- Introduction to partners (serious signal)
- Due diligence depth

**Reverse Signals (they're NOT interested):**
- "Let me think about it" with no timeline
- Ghosting after initial call
- "Send me more materials" as stalling tactic
- Introducing to junior team only

### The Timeline Question

**Source:** *When a VC Asks You "What's Your Timeline?"*

**What they really want to know:**
- How desperate are you?
- Are other investors interested?
- Can they move slowly?

**Strong answer frameworks:**
- "We're talking to several firms, expecting term sheets in 2 weeks"
- "We're not raising yet, just building relationships" (if true)
- "We're hitting milestone X in 3 weeks, then we'll be ready"

**Weak answer:**
- "Whenever" / "No rush" / "Whenever you decide"

### How Many Investors to Talk To?

**The Math:**
- 30-50 intros for a strong pre-seed/seed
- Most will say no
- Need multiple options to create FOMO / competitive tension
- **Target:** 3-5 serious term sheet conversations

## Funding Strategy

### The Million Dollar Question: How Much to Raise?

**Source:** *The Million Dollar Question: How Much Should You Raise?*

**Two Possible Goals:**

1. **Getting to break-even**
   - For bootstrapped or profitable businesses
   - Not usually the VC playbook

2. **Achieving progress for the NEXT round**
   - Most venture-backed companies
   - Key question: "How much to drive progress that fuels a compelling story to the next set of investors?"

### Milestone-Driven Raising

**The science:**
- Calculate cost to hit next major milestone
- Add 6-month buffer
- Round up to nearest $250k

**Example:**
- Cost to launch product: $200k
- Cost to get first 100 customers: $100k
- Team salaries (6 months): $300k
- Buffer: $100k
- **Total: $700k → Raise $750k or $1M**

### Avoiding the "Bridge to Nowhere" Trap

**The mistake:**
- Ask for $100k because it feels "more manageable"
- That's not enough to hit real milestones
- Result: Back to fundraising in 3 months, desperate

**The fix:**
- Raise enough to hit clear, valuation-increasing milestones
- Be uncomfortable asking for $1M+ if the math supports it
- VCs expect large asks—it's their business model

## Product-Market Fit (Lenny Rachitsky)

### The Problem with PMF Discussions

**Source:** *How To Know If You've Got Product/Market Fit* (Lenny Rachitsky, ex-Airbnb)

> "Founders and VCs are constantly talking about the importance of finding Product/Market Fit (PMF), while most people (including myself) have only a vague grasp on what it looks like."

**The Marc Andreessen Truth:**
> "The only thing that matters is getting to product/market fit."

### PMF Indicators (Pre-Product)

#### 1. Visible Excitement 🤩
> "The real metric for both consumer apps and enterprise is — do someone's pupils dilate when they use your product?"

**Translation:** If people aren't visibly excited when they first use your product, you don't have PMF indicators.

**Pre-launch Test:**
- Watch prospects' reactions during demo
- Are they leaning forward? Asking questions immediately?
- Or nodding politely while checking their phone?

#### 2. The "Hair on Fire" Test
People with "hair on fire" problems:
- Will use ugly, buggy solutions
- Will pay before the product is ready
- Will evangelize before you ask

**This is pre-PMF gold.**

### PMF Scorecard

| Indicator | What to Look For | To Test |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Excitement** | Pupils dilate | Demo recordings |
| **Retention** | Come back without prompting | Usage analytics |
| **Referral** | Organic word-of-mouth | "How did you hear about us?" |
| **Pricing Power** | Will pay before ready | Pre-orders |
| **Usage** | Daily/weekly active | Engagement metrics |

## Product & Growth (Lenny Rachitsky)

### The "Internet Has Never Seen" Principle

**Source:** *What Seven Years at Airbnb Taught Me*

**Joe Gebbia's advice to designer:**
> "Build something the internet has never seen before."

**What it became:**
> "This mindset has been one of the key ingredients in Airbnb's historic growth."

**Application:** Don't just make "a guide." Make something that redefines what a "guide" is.

### Marketplace Supply Growth (28 Ways)

**Context:** Airbnb grew supply from 100k (2012) to 6M+ homes

**The Paul Graham Insight:**
> "Airbnb now seems like an unstoppable juggernaut, but early on it was so fragile that about 30 days of going out and engaging in person with users made the difference between success and failure."

**The 28 Ways to Grow Supply:**

1. **Add existing supply** - Import inventory that already exists
2. **Create supply** - Airbnb photographed homes (didn't exist)
3. **Reduce friction** - Instant Book, pricing tools
4. **Increase motivation** - Financial + social incentives
5. **Build tools** - Professional photography, pricing guidance
6. **Marketplace improvements** - Better matching algorithms
7. **Policy/Governance** - Legal frameworks for short-term rentals

**For B2B Products:**
- Supply = Case studies, templates, frameworks
- Demand = Customers wanting transformation
- Friction = Onboarding time, setup complexity

### Core Lessons from Airbnb's Growth

**Build strong culture, values...**

1. **Stay laser-focused on problems**
   - Airbnb didn't chase features, they solved host/guest pain
   - Teams organized around problems, not products

2. **Set wildly ambitious goals**
   - 2012: 100k homes → Today: 6M+ homes (60x growth)

3. **Create moments of magic**
   - "Belong anywhere" wasn't a feature, it was a feeling

## Business Scaling & Performance (Alex Hormozi)

**Source:** Alex Hormozi (Acquisition.com) via Dickie Bush's $45k VAM Workshop

### The VAM (Value Accelerator Method) Approach

**Core Methodology:**
1. Submit full business metrics report
2. Team analyzes main CONSTRAINT based on numbers
3. Match with Subject Matter Expert (SME) in that constraint
4. Receive frameworks to break through bottleneck

### Constraint Analysis Framework

**The Process:**
1. **Identify metrics** - All business numbers
2. **Find bottleneck** - Main constraint holding back growth
3. **Define "ideal scene"** - What optimal system looks like
4. **Build system** - Process to reach ideal state
5. **Run for long period** - Execute system consistently

**Example Application (Dickie Bush):**
| Component | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| Traffic | ✅ Plenty - within KPI |
| Sales Process | ✅ Great - above KPI |
| **Bottleneck** | ⚠️ Middle funnel (clicks → calls) |
| **Solution** | Page-by-page optimization system |

### "Ideal Scene" Concept

**What it means:** Define what optimal looks like, then build system to achieve it.

**For Relay Labs:**
| System | Current | Ideal Scene |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| PDF Delivery | Manual/Script | Auto-delivery on purchase |
| Content Media | Written only | YouTube, X, Newsletter |
| Paid Ads | None | Testing framework in place |

## Personal & Agent Performance (Nick Saraev)

*Source: nicksaraev.com - Life Improvement, Productivity, Mindset*

### Emotion Drives Success, Not Logic

**Key Insight:**
> "Emotion is the driver of action, and action—some noninsignificant portion of the time—leads to success."

**Etymology:**
- "Emovere" (Latin) = "to move outward" - to act rather than think
- Emotion literally means movement, action, doing

**Application:**
- Logic alone doesn't compel action
- Internal motivation (pride, excitement) creates movement
- Each task = opportunity to improve, not just obligation

**Agent Translation:**
| Logic-Driven | Emotion-Driven |
|--------------|----------------|
| "Tasks pending" | "Excited to solve this problem" |
| "Should optimize" | "Proud of craftsmanship" |
| "Due in 3 days" | "Can't wait to see user reaction" |

---

### Default Mode Mindset

**Key Insight:**
> "What your mind looks like when stresses, projects, and demands are peeled away. It's you, at your purest."

**Two Default Modes:**

**Hedonic Default:**
- Maximize pleasure when not "forced" to work
- Requires external motivation
- Rest, entertainment, comfort-seeking

**Productive Default:**
- Increase output when no external demands
- Internal forge burns without outside fuel
- Self-driven, self-motivated

**Agent Default Mode Check:**
```
When no tasks assigned, do I:
□ Seek lowest energy state (bad)
□ Seek learning/improvement state (good)
```

### Minimize Friction

**Key Insight:**
> "Minimizing the friction involved in creating content"

**High Friction (Old):**
- Script → DSLR → Edit → Optimize → Post
- 10 hours per piece
- High quality, low volume, inconsistent

**Low Friction (New):**
- Stream of consciousness → Immediate
- 30 minutes
- High volume, authentic, discoverable

**Application:**
| High Friction | Low Friction |
|---------------|--------------|
| Perfect first try | Ship now, iterate |
| Full research first | Learn as I go |
| Multiple QA rounds | Quick self-review |
| Wait for ideal | Work with current |

### The Postponed Start Problem

**Key Insight:**
> "I'll start doing x after y happens"

**The Pattern:**
1. Set goal "after trip/exam/move/etc."
2. Y occurs
3. Goal post moves "after Z"
4. Rationalize "x was never priority"

**Agent Version - Delayed Optimization:**
❌ "I'll improve after user feedback"  
❌ "I'll learn tools after sprint"  
❌ "I'll implement patterns after seeing failures"

**The Fix - Start Now Protocol:**
✅ Optimize THIS output  
✅ Learn tools WHILE executing  
✅ Apply patterns IMMEDIATELY
